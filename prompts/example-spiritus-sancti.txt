BOTANICAL ACCURACY REQUIREMENTS

Critical identifying traits for Philodendron spiritus-sancti:

- Extremely long, narrow strap-like leaves with an acuminate tip — the most defining trait
- Leathery, coriaceous leaf texture — not thin or papery
- Leaf length-to-width ratio of approximately 10:1
- Long, terete petioles that are proportionally long relative to the blade
- Hemiepiphytic climbing growth habit (not self-heading)
- Glabrous leaves with subtle adaxial gloss (no pubescence)
- Lanceolate leaf shape — not cordate, not sagittate, not hastate

Morphology:

Leaf Shape:
Narrowly elliptic to lanceolate, long and strap-like with an acuminate apex; distinctly coriaceous (leathery) leaf blade

Leaf Ratio:
Extremely elongated, approximately 8:1 to 12:1 length-to-width ratio (leaves 60–90 cm long × 5–10 cm wide)

Texture:
Leathery (coriaceous), thick and stiff in texture, glabrous on both surfaces with a subtle glossy sheen adaxially

Petiole:
Long cylindrical petioles, terete in cross-section, proportionally long relative to the leaf blade (40–60 cm), pale green with faint longitudinal striations

Growth Habit:
Hemiepiphytic scandent (climbing) habit; stems elongate, rooting along internodes; can climb several meters on tree trunks

Mature Size:
Overall plant height 2–4 m in natural habitat; leaves 60–90 cm long, petioles 40–60 cm; stems 2–3 cm in diameter

Origin:
Espírito Santo, Brazil (critically endangered in its native range; restricted to a very small area of remaining Atlantic Forest)

Habitat:
Tropical moist lowland and montane forest; grows as a hemiepiphyte climbing on tree trunks in shaded understory with high humidity; well-drained organic-rich substrates; elevations from 200–800 m
